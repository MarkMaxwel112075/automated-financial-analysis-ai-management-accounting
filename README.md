# Automated Financial Analysis and Artificial Intelligence
### An Analysis of Strategic Decision-Making within Management Accounting Using Secondary Data

MSc Dissertation submitted to Brunel Business School, Brunel University London
Module: MG5636 (Dissertation) | Supervisor: Dr. Grigorios Theodosopoulos

---

## What this dissertation is about

Finance teams have spent the last decade bolting AI onto reporting and forecasting work, and the marketing around it tends to focus on what the technology can technically do — faster forecasts, automated dashboards, predictive models that outperform spreadsheets. What's had far less attention is the harder question: does any of this actually change how organisations make strategic decisions, and if so, under what conditions?

That's the gap this dissertation sits in. It looks specifically at Financial Automation Analysis (FAA) — AI-driven tools that generate budgets, forecasts, variance analysis and scenario models — and asks how and why it shapes strategic decision-making within management accounting, rather than just whether it speeds up reporting.

## Why secondary data, not interviews

The honest answer is access. CFOs and senior finance professionals are difficult to get meaningful interview time with, and a single researcher running a master's dissertation doesn't have the reach to build a primary dataset large enough to say anything generalisable. So instead, this study treats existing published material — peer-reviewed journal articles, professional body reports (ACCA, Deloitte, KPMG, PwC, OECD/BCG/INSEAD), and a couple of organisational case studies (Sainsbury's and Microsoft, Grant Thornton) — as the evidence base, and analyses it against established theory rather than simply summarising it.

Forty-three sources made it into the final analysis after screening an initial pool of 58. The approach isn't a systematic literature review in the formal sense — the goal wasn't to catalogue everything written on the topic, but to select material that could meaningfully speak to the theoretical questions being asked.

## The theoretical lens

Three frameworks do the analytical work here, each covering a different part of the picture:

**Decision Theory**, specifically the idea of bounded rationality, explains why automation gets framed as useful in the first place — humans can't process the volume of data modern finance functions generate, so AI tools step in to fill that gap. But the literature is clear this isn't a clean win: training data carries its own biases, and more analytical power doesn't automatically translate into better judgment.

**Socio-Technical Systems Theory** is what stops this from being a purely technical story. It treats FAA adoption as a change to the social fabric of an organisation — roles, skills, norms — not just a new tool plugged into existing workflows. This is where the shift from "accountant as reporter" to "accountant as advisor" gets its theoretical grounding.

**Dynamic Capabilities** closes the loop by asking whether any of this actually makes an organisation more responsive. Having better analytics doesn't guarantee strategic agility — that depends on whether the organisation can sense, seize and reconfigure around what the analytics tell it.

## The four propositions

The literature review builds toward four testable propositions, and the findings chapter is structured around checking the secondary evidence against each one:

- **P1** — Decision quality improves with FAA only when paired with active human oversight, not as a replacement for it.
- **P2** — Trust in FAA, and therefore its adoption, is moderated by how solid the governance around it is (explainability, audit trails, clear accountability).
- **P3** — The strategic value FAA creates is mediated by professional transformation — accountants have to actually develop into the advisory role, it doesn't happen automatically.
- **P4** — FAA contributes to organisational agility, but only when it's embedded in aligned governance and capability structures, not bolted on as a standalone tool.

All four held up reasonably well across the evidence, with the consistent caveat running through every section: the technology is rarely the limiting factor. Governance maturity and whether people are actually using the outputs critically matter more.

## What the evidence actually shows

A few patterns worth flagging directly:

Forecasting accuracy improvements in the 10–25% range turn up repeatedly across professional reports and academic studies, alongside reductions of up to 20% in budgetary variance. These numbers should be read as indicative rather than as proof of a causal effect — they come from different organisations, different measurement approaches, and different stages of FAA maturity, so treating them as a single clean statistic would overstate what the evidence supports.

Where governance was weak, two failure modes kept showing up: either staff over-relied on the automated output without questioning it, or they rejected the tool outright. Neither produced good decisions. Organisations with formal AI governance policies — explainable AI, audit trails, defined accountability — saw meaningfully higher usage of these tools.

On the professional side, the shift away from routine reporting toward strategic and advisory work is well documented, but it's uneven. It depends heavily on whether the organisation has actually invested in training people to interpret and contextualise what the automation produces, rather than assuming the capability shows up on its own.

## Methodological position, briefly

The study sits on critical realism (organisational phenomena are real but only imperfectly observable through proxies like forecast accuracy) and a pragmatic epistemology, since the analysis pulls in both quantitative indicators and qualitative, contextual material. It's cross-sectional rather than longitudinal — a deliberate trade-off given the time constraints of a master's project, acknowledged directly in the limitations section rather than glossed over.
